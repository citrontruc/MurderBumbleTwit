## Déroulé

Pour répondre à la question centrale : oui, Lord BumbleTwit est un arnaqueur. Par contre, il y a bien un magicien présent lors de la soirée. Il s'agit en réalité de l'inspecteur Lazare qui possède la capacité de faire revenir temporairement les morts à la vie. S'il a fait cela, c'est parce qu'il voit dans la résurrection de Lord BumbleTwit un moyen de résoudre une bonne fois pour toute le mystère autour de la mort de son bon ami Lord Bertham.

Lord BumbleTwit ne voulait hélas pas lacher le morceau et se doutait (à raison) que c'était parce qu'un des coupables était quelqu'un de proche de Lord Bertham. Convaincu qu'il s'agissait de son cousin Pierce Bertham (qui n'a d'ailleurs jamais caché sa colère vis à vis du peu qu'il a obtenu dans le testament de Lord Bertham), Lord BumbleTwit imagine la mise en scène grotesque qui mènera à sa mort. S'il connait bien l'occulte, il sait aussi que des charlatans mal intentionnés l'utilisent pour soutirer l'argent des crédules. Ils connaissent des techniques pour faire croire que les morts communiquent avec eux et possèdent des outils pour simuler la voix des morts. Lors de ces visites de courtoisie, Lord BumbleTwit est d'ailleurs repéré par Aiden Rapier et Gideon Judas qui se mettent à avoir des doutes sur la véracité des expériences de Lord BumbleTwit.

L'idée de BumbleTwit est la suivante : il va utiliser les techniques de ces imposteurs afin de simuler la présence de Lord Bertham. Le secret de la mise en scène tient à l'ambiance de mystère qui doit se créer : les lumières seraient tamisées, ce qui cacherait les manipulations du medium, la table autour de laquelle les gens vont se réunir a été couvert d'une nappe trop longue afin de masquer les mouvements des jambes et Lord BumbleTwit peut ainsi la déplacer à son aise (une inspection de la table peut d'ailleurs révéler la supercherie). L'empreinte sur la fenêtre a été faite discrètement avec de l'huile au préalable. Pour la faire apparaitre, il fallait refroidir le verre afin de créer de la condensation. C'est là que le complice de Lord BumbleTwit rentre en scène : il s'agit de son meilleur ami Tybalt le plaisantin. Celui-ci avait vu dans toute la séance l'occasion de faire une bonne farce aux autres participants et a accepté promptement de participer à la réception.

Edgar Vale never called himself a medium. The word sounded provincial to him, vulgar even, like something attached to women in lace gloves whispering to widows in rented parlors. He preferred engineer. Mechanist. Director of atmospheric conditions.

His séance room occupied the upper floor of a converted townhouse whose original architecture he had quietly mutilated over the course of eleven years. Guests saw velvet drapery, dark walnut, and ecclesiastical clutter. What existed behind the walls was a compact machine for manufacturing belief.

The room itself was oval by design. Vale had discovered early that corners gave confidence to skeptical minds; people unconsciously mapped space through edges and right angles. The oval denied certainty. Shadows accumulated unevenly. Distances became difficult to judge.

At the center stood the séance table: ash wood, circular, polished to a near-black sheen. Visitors often remarked on how heavy it felt. In reality it was extraordinarily light. The pedestal was hollow, the underside ribbed like the shell of a drum to amplify vibration. Inside the central column ran three mechanisms.

The first was a weighted pendulum mounted on gimbals. A concealed foot treadle beneath Vale’s chair could set it swinging. Once in motion, the pendulum transferred rhythmic force into the table structure. To participants resting their fingertips lightly upon the surface, the vibration felt organic — hesitant at first, then insistent, as though responding to questions.

The second mechanism was pneumatic. Vale maintained compressed air in copper reservoirs hidden beneath the floorboards. Thin rubber tubes traveled upward through the table leg and terminated in tiny ports concealed among the wood carvings. At precise moments he could depress brass valves under the armrests of his chair, releasing faint currents of cold air across the hands and necks of the sitters. Human beings interpreted unexplained cold immediately as presence. The association was nearly universal.

The third mechanism existed solely for sound.

Within the hollow column sat a rotating wooden cylinder studded with irregular teeth. When engaged by another hidden pedal, it scraped against taut wire reeds mounted internally. The effect resembled distant creaking floorboards or muted knocks from within the walls. Because the chamber had been deliberately designed with asymmetric acoustics, nobody could locate the source correctly. Vale had consulted a theater architect years before to ensure reflected sound arrived milliseconds apart from multiple directions.

The windows were equally fraudulent.

Each pane had been treated in advance using an almost invisible mixture of lanolin and mineral oil applied with etched rubber molds. During ordinary conditions the glass appeared clean. But concealed steam pipes embedded beneath the window frame could warm the surrounding air suddenly while the outer glass remained cold from the night air. Condensation bloomed across the surface — except where the oils resisted moisture.

Fingerprints appeared first.

Then whole palms.

On rare evenings, a child-sized handprint.

Vale timed these manifestations carefully. Never immediately after discussion of the dead. The human mind resisted direct causality when too obvious. Instead he allowed conversation to drift elsewhere before activating the steam release with a pressure switch hidden beneath the carpet.

People remembered miracles more vividly when they believed they had discovered them themselves.

The lighting system was his proudest achievement.

No lamp in the room was stationary. Every source of illumination could be altered subtly through weighted shutters operated from a narrow control corridor behind the walls. During séances, illumination never diminished evenly. Certain corners darkened faster than others. Faces became isolated while hands disappeared. The human visual cortex, deprived of stable reference points, compensated by inventing continuity where none existed.

Vale exploited this relentlessly.

A woman would swear she saw her dead husband standing near the curtains when in fact she had only observed the partial silhouette of another guest distorted by angled shadow and expectation.

For stronger manifestations, he used projection.

Not modern projection — too sharp, too mechanical — but a modified nineteenth-century reflection system adapted from stage illusions. Hidden behind black gauze at one end of the chamber stood a narrow alcove containing a lantern and movable painted figures mounted on articulated glass. At precise angles, faint translucent images reflected onto a specially treated pane embedded invisibly within the room’s architecture.

The ghosts never appeared solid.

That was deliberate.

Sharp detail invited scrutiny. Ambiguity invited imagination.

Vale understood that the audience completed the illusion themselves. His apparatus merely suggested the impossible. The sitters performed the remainder internally.

Movement throughout the room depended heavily on darkness discipline. Vale rehearsed darkness the way musicians rehearsed scales. He knew precisely how many steps separated his chair from each wall. Thin ridges had been carved into the floorboards beneath the carpet, allowing him to orient himself by touch through his shoes. Small magnets sewn into the cuffs of his sleeves enabled him to locate hidden iron fixtures instantly in total blackness.

The floating objects relied on a network of blackened silk lines stretched near the ceiling. During daylight the system was visible from certain angles, but under dim red illumination it vanished completely. Tiny pulleys scavenged from clockmaking equipment allowed objects to glide with almost no audible friction. Bells rang. Cabinets opened. A dead child’s doll crossed the room slowly enough to avoid detection but quickly enough to bypass rational analysis.

The voices required assistants.

There were always assistants.

One occupied a crawlspace between the walls accessible through a concealed hatch behind the library shelves downstairs. The crawlspace connected to speaking tubes terminating behind decorative vents around the séance chamber. By whispering through linen-covered diaphragms, assistants produced voices that seemed both near and directionless.

The effect on grieving parents was devastating.

Vale selected assistants carefully. They needed emotional detachment and absolute silence outside performances. Most importantly, they needed to understand timing. A supernatural event delivered too early generated suspicion. Delivered too late, it lost force.

Everything depended on cadence.

He treated séances less like rituals and more like orchestral compositions. Tension. Release. Silence. Crescendo.

Even the table touches had methodology behind them.

Beneath his fingernails Vale occasionally wore thin caps of vulcanized rubber coated in phosphorescent compound. After extinguishing the lamps entirely, he could brush a sitter’s wrist and leave behind a faint glowing residue that lingered for several seconds. Participants interpreted this as spiritual energy or contact.

Once, for an especially wealthy industrialist, Vale constructed an entire false haunting.

The client’s deceased daughter had supposedly loved music. During the séance, a music box hidden within the wall began playing her favorite lullaby. Simultaneously, concealed electromagnets behind the plaster activated in sequence, causing framed photographs to tremble subtly.

The man wept immediately.

What he never understood was that Vale had spent weeks preparing the performance. Servants had been bribed. Household schedules documented. Samples of the daughter’s handwriting copied from discarded letters. Even the lullaby had been recovered by interviewing former nursery staff under false pretenses.

People imagined frauds succeeded through clever machinery alone.

Vale knew better.

The machinery merely stabilized belief.

The real engine was grief.

Every mechanism in the room ultimately served a psychological principle: give the witness partial information under emotional stress and the mind will construct the miracle voluntarily.

By the end of each séance, guests rarely remembered specific events accurately. They remembered impressions. A cold hand. A voice behind them. A face in fogged glass. A table trembling beneath their fingertips.

And afterward, in the safety of gaslight and ordinary architecture, the impossibility of it all became proof.

Surely, they told themselves, such experiences could not have been manufactured.

Edgar Vale depended entirely on that sentence.
